About the Creator

Manuni Bhavsar

Explorer · Historian · Technologist

I am Manuni Bhavsar, a passionate explorer of ancient history and emerging technologies. My journey began not in a classroom but through wonder — the same kind of wonder that drives archaeologists to crawl through jungle undergrowth or dive into submerged ruins.

I was profoundly inspired by Albert Lin, National Geographic Explorer-at-Large, whose groundbreaking use of LiDAR, AI, and satellite imaging has fundamentally transformed how we find and study lost civilisations. His philosophy — that technology should extend our senses, not replace human curiosity — resonates deeply with everything I create.

This website is my tribute to the cities that shaped humanity and the tools that are bringing them back to light. Every card, every map, every myth recorded here is a step toward making these stories accessible to all.

Inspired by Albert Lin & His Technologies

Albert Lin, PhD, is a researcher, engineer, and National Geographic Explorer who has spent over a decade combining cutting-edge technology with archaeological exploration. His work discovering Genghis Khan's tomb, mapping Angkor's hidden suburbs, and locating Maya mega-cities under the Guatemalan canopy has redefined modern archaeology.

His core belief: "We don't have to dig to discover." By combining remote sensing, crowd-sourcing, and machine learning, Lin makes exploration non-destructive and globally collaborative.

FEATURED TECHNOLOGY

LiDAR — Seeing Through Jungles

Light Detection and Ranging fires 400,000 laser pulses per second from aircraft. Pulses that slip through jungle canopy gaps bounce off the ground, building a bare-earth elevation model that reveals hidden cities, roads, and reservoirs invisible to the human eye or conventional satellite.

Albert Lin — Timeline of Discovery

A Decade of Breakthroughs

2008 — The Search for Genghis Khan's Tomb

Lin led the Valley of the Khans Project in Mongolia, using satellite imagery and crowdsourced analysis from 10,000+ volunteers online. This was one of the first large-scale citizen science archaeological surveys, producing results without disturbing a single centimetre of sacred Mongolian soil.

2014 — LiDAR over Angkor Wat

The Khmer Archaeology LiDAR Consortium flew LiDAR sensors over the jungle surrounding Angkor Wat, revealing a previously unknown medieval city of hundreds of square kilometres — instantly making it the largest pre-industrial urban settlement ever discovered. Lin's own remote-sensing work used similar LiDAR techniques at sites like Ciudad Perdida in Colombia.

2018 — Maya Mega-City, Guatemala

Lin presented the PACUNAM LiDAR Initiative's findings in a National Geographic special, showcasing the discovery of over 60,000 previously unknown Maya structures beneath the Guatemalan jungle — including pyramids, causeways, and irrigation systems — through an augmented-reality visualization of the survey data.

2019 — Petra's Hidden Origins, Jordan

In an episode of "Lost Cities with Albert Lin," Lin investigated Petra's Nabataean origins, tracking down evidence of cities and temples that revealed the famous rock-cut city was part of a far larger ancient network than previously understood.

2022 — AI-Assisted Site Detection

Lin's teams began integrating machine-learning algorithms trained on known archaeological sites to automatically flag potential ruins in satellite imagery across vast regions of the Amazon and Sahara, reducing survey time from decades to weeks.
